Listening to Wes Montgomery – A 10-Step Plan

Not about playing fast. Just learning how to really hear what Wes is doing, one small step at a time.

  1. Pick one album. Choose a single record (for example Smokin’ at the Half Note). For the next weeks, listen mostly to this one album so your ear has something familiar to grow in.
  2. First listen: just enjoy. Play the album once without analysis. No guitar, no notes, no pausing. Let it be background to making tea or sitting on the sofa.
  3. Notice his sound. On the next listen, focus only on tone: the warm thumb attack, how round or sharp the notes are, how loud he is compared to the band.
  4. Follow the melody. Choose one tune. Hum or whistle along with the main melody, even badly. The goal is simply to be able to recognise that tune when it comes back.
  5. Trace the time feel. Listen to how Wes places his notes against the drums and bass. Tap your foot softly. Notice when he sits right on the beat, slightly behind, or pushes ahead.
  6. Listen for phrases, not licks. Pick one solo and hear where the musical sentences start and stop. Count how many bars a phrase lasts. You don’t need theory words—just “short”, “medium”, “long” phrases is enough.
  7. Sing one short line. Find a tiny fragment of a solo you like and sing it with the recording: “da-da-da…” Matching pitch is optional; matching the shape and rhythm is the point.
  8. Compare two versions. If a tune appears on more than one album, listen to both. Notice what stays the same (melody, basic feel) and what changes (tempo, introductions, solos).
  9. Keep a tiny listening log. After each focused listen, write one or two lines: “Track 3 – loved the octave solo,” or “He left so much space on the ballad.” This keeps your ear awake.
  10. Return over weeks, not days. Come back to the same album many times over a month or two. The goal isn’t to “finish” Wes—it’s to let his sound become a quiet reference point in your own musical listening.

No transcriptions are required. If you end up reaching for your guitar, that’s fine—but simply listening like this is already serious practice.
