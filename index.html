10-Step Listening Plan

Learning to Listen to Wes Montgomery

Not about speed or theory. Just training your ear to notice tone, time, and phrasing — one album, many listens.

Start with one record

Choose a single album (for example Smokin’ at the Half Note) and stay with it for a while. Let it become somewhere your ears “live”, not just visit.

One album Repeat listens No hurry
1
Pick one album and commit. For a month or two, make this your main Wes record. Familiarity is part of the practice: knowing roughly what’s coming makes details easier to hear.
2
First listen: no analysis. Play the album through without stopping. No guitar, no pausing, no “what scale is this?” — just let it colour the room while you sit, cook, or stare out of the window.
3
Listen only for sound. On another day, focus purely on tone: the rounded thumb attack, how notes bloom and decay, how his sound sits against the cymbals and bass.
4
Follow the melody. Choose one tune. Hum or whistle the head, even clumsily. The aim is simply to recognise it instantly when it returns — like recognising a friend’s voice in a crowd.
5
Feel the time, not the theory. Tap your foot or lightly on your leg. Notice whether Wes leans on the beat, behind it, or ahead of it. This “micro-timing” is half the music.
6
Hear phrases, not licks. Pick one solo. Listen for where the musical sentences begin and end. Are they short questions? Long paragraphs? You don’t need bar counts — just “short / medium / long” in your own words.
7
Sing one small line with him. Find a tiny fragment you love and sing along with the recording. Matching the exact pitches is optional; matching the shape and rhythm is already deep work.
8
Compare two versions of a tune. If you find the same song on another album, listen to both in one sitting. Notice what stays: the basic melody, the harmonic shape — and what changes: tempo, introductions, endings, solo mood.
9
Keep a tiny listening note. After a focused listen, jot a single line: “Track 3 – octaves felt like waves,” or “Ballad solo: so much space.” This helps your memory notice what it’s learning.
10
Return over weeks, not days. Come back to the same record again and again. The goal isn’t to “finish” Wes — it’s to let his sound quietly re-tune how you hear guitar, time, and melody.

You can do all of this without ever picking up a guitar. If one day you feel like reaching for it, fine — but even if you never do, this kind of listening is already serious musical practice.
